Introduction: A Vague Idea

The Immune System is defined as a network of biological processes that protect an organism from diseases. It responds to a wide variety of threats such as viruses and parasites. Many species tend to have 2 major subsystems of the immune system. First one being the innate immune system and second being the adaptive immune system. This trend is mostly found in vertebrates.

Innate Immune System: The Old Method

The Innate Immune System is an older evolutionary method, and is the dominant immune system response among plants, fungi, insects and primitive multicellular organisms. Some of the major functions include:

Recruitment of immune cells to infection sites by producing chemical mediators such as cytokines.

Activates the Complement Cascade to identify bacteria and promote clearance of antibody complexes or dead cells.

Identification of foreign substances present in organs, tissues, blood and lymph, done by specialized white blood cells.

Activates the adaptive immune system.

Acts as a physical barrier by forming clotts to prevent further invasion of foreign microbes.

Adaptive Immune System

This immune sub-system mainly consists of 2 specialized cells that eliminate pathogens or prevent them from growing. The difference between Innate Immune System and Adaptive Immune System being that while the Innate Immune System is pre-programmed or already prepared for certain microbial threats, the Adaptive Immune System is very specific. It uses immunological memory after having encountered a specific pathogen. In simpler terms, the Adaptive Immune System remembers the structure of the pathogens which it has previously encountered. Antibodies are a very important part of the Adaptive Immune System. This sub-system can provide long-lasting protection, sometimes for as long as a person's lifetime. For eg. A person who gets infected from measels and recovers, that person is protected against measels for their entire life; in other cases, it may not provide lifetime protection. For eg. Chickenpox. The process of Adaptive Immune System is the basis of vaccination. The cells carrying out the Adaptive Immune Response are white blood cells known as lymphocytes. B cells and T cells are two such lymphocytes carry out some important tasks: Antibody response and cell mediated response.

Sub-Topic: Antibody Response

In Antibody Response, the B cells activate and secrete/release antibodies, and antibodies as we know it are basically proteins known as immunoglobulins. These antibodies move throughout the bloodstream and bind/anchor to foreign antigen (Antigens are basically substances that trigger the adaptive immune system). This response causes the antigen to deactivate. Sometimes this is the reason behind hayfever, asthma or any allergy because the antibodies can deactivate harmless antigens.

In Adaptive Immune System, receptors specific to certain pathogens are acquired/gained during the lifetime of an organism, whereas, in Innate Immune System pathogen-specific receptors are already encoded in the genome. This allows the Adaptive Immune System to be prepared for future challenges.
